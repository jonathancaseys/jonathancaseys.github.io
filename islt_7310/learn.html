Let's Learn about Sound: Vibration

When we talk about sound vibrations, its usually something that we feel or hear. It is not often something that we can see. Whether its the strike of a snare drum or the strum of the strings of a guitar, usually what we can see is blurry movement, but not individual vibrations. In the clip to the left, the band Metallica teams up with a video crew to record slow motion video of their perfomance, including drums, bass, piano, and guitar.

Here, the video crew is using close-up footage using high-speed cameras with an extrememly high framerate to capture individual vibrations that we can now see with the naked eye!

In the second clip, I have created some of these same effects using a smartphone and some simple instruments. I first use my camera to record a tuning fork being dipped into water, and then I record my ukulele strings being strummed.

Let's Learn about Sound: Properties of Strings

It can be common for young students to associate small instruments with "low" sounds, and large instruments with "high" sounds, simply because sometimes we associate low with small and high with big. But if we know any basics about the properties of strings, then we can learn a little bit about the relationship between the qualities of a string and its pitch.

In the infographic I have created to the left, we can see a graphic representation of a "standing wave", or the type of sound wave created by a string instrument. We can see that it moves back and forth, shaking widely at some points and narrowly at others. The next image shows a zoomed in ukuele, where we can see the vibrations but not specifically the back and forth motion that we saw in the first lesson with the slow-motion camera.
So why do some instruments make higher sounds and other instruments lower sounds? It heavily depends on the length of the strings. A long instrument, like the string bass in the picture, makes very low sounds, while the violin, with its short strings, makes very high sounds.
And then we have instruments that make both high and low sounds, like the autoharp in the bottom left.

Cymatics: Visualization of Sound

Students will gain a better understanding of the properties of sound waves through the use of cymatics, or visualizations of sound. This can be done through various mediums, such as chladni plates, water, magnetic liquids, fire, and electricity. In the case of a chladni plate, for example, a steel plate is connected to a tone generator. By covering the plate with sand or salt, the experimenter adjusts the tone generator until a resonant frequency is found. When this happens, the particles will form geometric patterns on the plate, as seen in this image to the left. The more complex the pattern, the higher the pitch, and the less complex, the lower the pitch. Watch this video here to see how cymatic patterns are used to show the properties of sound waves.
